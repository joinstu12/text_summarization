[1]First define disability. [2]Someone can be disAbled by a congenital defect, chronic illnesses or injury. [3]It could be any combination of, or even multiple, congenital defects, illnesses and/or injuries. [4]There are also debilitating side effects of medications. [5]A "nonreader" is not necessarily blind [6] they may have had a TBI (traumatic brain injury) or have a Learning Disability. [7]Disabilities can effect fine and gross motor skills. [8]A Quadriplegic may use different assistive technologies, such as voice pilots, mouth tools or devices that follow eye movement. [9]IMO, before you can define Accessibility you must first define disability. [10]Why? [11]I view that anyone should be able to access information, regardless of whether or not they have a "disability." [12]Regardless if we call that a "disability" or not, it should not interfere with your ability to access content on the Internet. [13]I think it's perfectly reasonable to define "accessibility" with having a strict definition of "disability" as long as your accessibility definition doesn't speak _only_ about access for people who fit that disability definition. [14]A disability is a consequence of design flaws in the environment. [15]I like to tell people that if a blind person can't access the web page she's trying to use, the REASON is not _her_ disability, but the disability of the _web author_ who created the page. [16]That web author is "disabled" because he was _not able_ to make a correct web page. [17]Why should she suffer for _his_ problem or failing? [18]In no way is a user with a disability "disabled" on the web [19] the sites THEMSELVES have the disability inherent in their code by not being accessible! [20]We should identify the problem where it exists. [21]The problem is not that there's anything "wrong with" the user, it is a problem with the page author and his output! [22]I should like to add that we do have to accept that the victorians probably had an insuperable task in trying to design and build a wheelchair for a quadraplegic that the individual could control themselves.
